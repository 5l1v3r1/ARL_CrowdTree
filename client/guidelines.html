2. Examples

2.1 Subjects, objects, and complements of copula

The following examples show how to deal with subjects, direct object, indirect objects, and complements of copula (verbs like be).

John is tall.
The goalie gave him the ball.
The sandwiches were eaten quickly

2.2 Object complements / small clauses

The idea is to attach both the object and its complement to the verb. Don't worry about differences between verbs like promise, persuade, tell.

I told him to go.
They helped them build .
We saw them eating.

2.3 Determiners, quantifiers, and possessives

This section covers words like the, a, my, your, all, and both.

The dog ate them all.
All his siblings sang some songs.
Sally's two cats were both
growling like lions.

2.4 Adjectives, names, numbers, & compound nouns

These examples show how to handle adjectives, names, numbers, and compound nouns.

The two old soup pot covers had 16 dents each.
The big TV is heavier than this .
Mr. Tom T. Thomson Sr. owns ABCsoft Inc.
This is not as heavy as that .

2.5 Adverbs and negation

There are many adverbs in English and not all of them behave the same. Below are some examples.

One of the more difficult cases may be when the adverb modifies the verb but the verb has modals/auxillaries (e.g., can, be, have, do) like have been eaten. In these cases, please just attach the adverb to the closest verb in the verb chain. If more than one of the verbs is closest, just attach it to the one that makes the most sense to you. If they both make just as much sense, please attach it to the one on the right.

Sadly , the brightly glowing monster very easily
defeated the extremely brave knights.
Years ago, this wasn't farmland.

2.6 Prepositions, subordinating conjunctions, particles

Prepositions and subordinating conjunctions establish relationships between different parts of the sentence. For example, they can describe when or where something is or occurred.

Examples of these kinds of words are in, on, up, down, before, after, and if.

After we ate, the people from Dreamland
talked with us.
I said that it was true before I really knew.
They broke the fight up soon after it started.
Note: The up in break up doesn't act like a preposition here.
Since up could be put on either side of fight,
fight should connect to break, not up.

2.7 Verb chains / modal verbs / auxiliary verbs

This section shows how to treat chains of verbs consisting of a main verb and one or more helper verbs such as can, could, will, would, be, have, or do.

We have been swimming.
The cookies can be eaten.
The letters are being flown here.

2.8 Coordinating conjunctions

Coordinating conjunctions are words like and, or, nor, and but. The words yet, so, and for can also be coordinating conjunctions. The examples below show you how to treat coordinating conjunctions, both when they are in the middle of a sentence and when they are at the beginning. Also, note that the words either, neither, and both sometimes occur with coordinating conjunctions.

John and Mary
live in a cottage.
And there was music.
Either it will rain or it will snow.
I ran to the store, bought some apples and oranges,
and then ran home.

2.9 Apposition

Apposition involves putting two things side by side. An apposed phrase can be enclosed by punctuation such as commas, parentheses, or dashes.

John, their brother, looked on quietly.
The cost is only 1500 yen ($14).
The spaceship -- the biggest ever --
loomed large.

2.10 The word to

The word to frequently precedes both verbs and nouns (as well as other words). Please treat these cases similarly with the word to on top as seen below.

The roses are
pleasant to smell.
I went
to the store
to buy food.
He ate it
for them to see.
Please handle for ... to ... this way.

2.11 The word that

The word that shows up in a few different places, so we've given it its own section. We suggest you take a look at the example below to get an idea of its different uses. For the curious, you may also wish to look at the sections covering relative clauses, determiners, and subordinating conjunctions.

Mikie ate the lollipop
that Thomas bought.
I saw
that they were terrified.
Don't buy that --
buy that one!

2.12 Questions, relative clauses, and WH- words (e.g., who, what, when)

This section shows how to deal with questions and relative clauses. These are often headed by WH- words such as who, what, and where.

Who is on third?
The man who bought it ate it where he lives.
Why did you go?
I asked him how he found it.
What he saw perplexed him.
Do you know the muffin man?
The house I loved was too expensive.

2.13 Speech acts

Direct speech is often split into pieces or comes before the verb. The speech verb is the root of the sentence.

"I believe," said John, "that it is fake."
The board has been reduced, analysts reported.

2.14 Clefts and it extraposition

Clefts and it extraposition can be confusing to annotate. Please see the examples below.

It is strange that he could not come.
It was John who didn't understand.
That made it impossible to eat.

2.15 Dollar amounts and percentages

The key thing to remember with dollar amounts is to tree the dollar sign ($) as the head.

I took out a $1 million loan.
About 7.5% of the company is publicly traded.

2.16 Final remarks / examples of a couple other things

These guidelines cover most of what you will encounter but don't cover all possible situations. If you can't find an example that covers the situation you are dealing with, please make your best effort, keeping in mind these guidelines.

I like ice cream -- can you tell?
I invited John; sadly, his parents would not let him come.
They arrived on March 15th, 2000 from Kalamazoo, MI, USA.
About 100 people came out to see the nearly 600 elephants.
A toothpick in his mouth, Mike sauntered into the kitchen, humming quietly.
Unfazed by the earlier rain, the spider climbed up the water spout, singing as it went.
His eyes wide, adventurer John S. Smith leapt across the growing crevice.
